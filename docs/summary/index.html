Research summary

Why unsealed trash and unrinsed recycling is a health issue, not just an annoyance.

This page summarizes peer-reviewed research on fruit flies as vectors of foodborne pathogens. All sources are publicly accessible via PubMed and linked below.

Finding 01 — Breeding

Fruit flies breed directly in the sources we create.

Sticky residue in bottles and cans, and rotting food scraps, are prime egg-laying sites. A single breeding source — one unrinsed bottle, one unsealed bag — can sustain a full infestation. Once established, flies move freely between the bin area and any exposed food in the building.

Finding 02 — Transmission

E. coli · Salmonella · Listeria

Fruit flies mechanically transfer these pathogens onto food.

Lab studies confirm that flies pick up bacteria at contaminated sites and deposit them directly onto food surfaces on subsequent landings. This is called mechanical transmission — no biting required, contact alone is sufficient.

Pava-Ripoll et al., 2018 ↗

Finding 03 — Contamination rate

50%

Of food samples showed measurable E. coli contamination after fly contact.

Field data found contamination in half of all food samples where flies had landed, with over 600 colony-forming units (CFU) per landing event. One landing from one fly is a meaningful exposure.

PMC5928814 ↗

Finding 04 — Persistence

7 days

Bacteria survive inside the fly for at least a week after a single exposure.

It's not just surface contamination on legs and wings — bacteria survive internally for at least 7 days after a single exposure. A fly that visited the bin area last week is still a potential carrier today.

Sela et al., PMC1169043 ↗
